Spreading ideas

The most dangerous ideas are the ones that feel right immediately. When someone presents you with a view that slides perfectly into your mental model, that's not validation — it's a red flag. What spreads isn't what's true, but what fits people's existing beliefs.

This explains a lot of otherwise puzzling phenomena. It explains why smart people can hold obviously wrong views about complex conflicts based purely on their background. It explains why your uncle is so confident about everything. It explains racism. Ideas spread not because they're correct, but because they're easy to believe and share.

The real tragedy is that everyone thinks they're being logical. But if you find yourself unable to steel-man your opponent's position — to explain their view in terms that they would agree with — you're probably the one missing something. This is especially true when you notice your views align suspiciously well with your demographic group. The more an idea confirms what you already believe, the more carefully you should examine it.

Consider how ideas spread on social media. A complex analysis of the Israel-Palestine conflict will get far fewer shares than a tweet saying "X group is clearly evil." The latter spreads not because it's true, but because it's easy to understand and matches what certain groups already believe.

QVC sells products by matching people's intuitive but incorrect models of how things work. A water filter commercial shows red dots pulsing in someone's head, then cool water washing over their brain, "curing" the headache. It works because it maps perfectly to how headaches feel.

In each case, what matters isn't truth — it's how easily the idea slots into existing beliefs. The more an idea confirms what a group already thinks, the faster it spreads within that group. That's why the same political arguments tend to cluster by demographic. It's not that different groups have different access to facts. It's that they have different models of what makes sense.
