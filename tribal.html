It is much easier to identify incorrect opinions than correct ones. If someone says their stock trading strategy is informed by their morning mood, a better trading strategy isn’t required to know theirs is flawed.

Humans like to understand how things work using simplified models built using interactions and intuitions. Most people don’t fully understand how a refrigerator works -– they just understand how it will behave in different situations using their simplified model of it. The model serves its purpose well most of the time, and allows for effective usage of the fridge.

Simplified models miss a lot of important details, and often make incorrect extrapolations. If Tribe X and Tribe Y are in conflict, members form models of both tribes informed by information around them. When Tribe X acts, the act is seen through different models. Models use shortcuts, fill in blanks, and extrapolate. We are left with very different motivations and interpretations of the same action, and a worsening conflict.

If an opinion is shared by people with a common prior experience (which informed their model, like race, religion, gender, etc) the model they share is likely biased. Thinking about what priors those who share an opinion have can help identify biases.

Opinions make a lot of sense to those that hold them. If an opinion is labeled dismissively (stupid, evil, etc), the labeler is missing a lot of information and taking lazy shortcuts. Productive discourse is marked with a desire to understand the other point of view. It is also requires all parties to be open to changing their mind.
