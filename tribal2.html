Disagreeing poorly

It is difficult to watch groups fight over an opinion when we see both points of view from the outside. It seems obvious where all parties are coming from – why don’t they see it? Often, we find ourselves in one of the parties, feeling attacked, misunderstood, and patronized. Often, we don’t even realize we are returning the favor to the poor sod on the other end of our vitriol. They just don’t fucking get it.

As a Kashmiri with Indian friends, a highschool PC gamer with die-hard console friends, a Muslim with Jewish friends, a former League of Legends addict, I’ve seen discourse become problematic from many angles. With 24/7 access to discourse, short circuiting and avoiding shitty disagreements is more important than ever.

When we identify an opinion as incorrect or biased, there is often pressure to clarify our stance. If I find out you trade stocks based on the phase of the moon, I do not need a more correct trading strategy to discard yours, and save my brainspace from pollution. I will simply ignore your strategy, and live a better life for it. We should feel empowered to discard incoming opinions without having a better one of our own.

If an opinion is shared by a group of people with a strong shared prior (religion, race, gender, ideology), there is likely a lot of shared bias. In this way, it is also useful to observe what the characteristics of those that you share an opinion with are. If you think Javascript is the best programming language, and so do all your classmates from your Javascript bootcamp – something’s fishy. This form of identification is helpful to me on issues where priors play a huge role, like the Palestine conflict.

Most people don’t actually understand how a fridge works – they thought up a simplified model of a fridge using their interactions with fridges. This model serves its purpose well, and their food stays fresh. Simplified models miss a lot of very important details. When two people disagree about how much energy is wasted keeping the fridge door open for too long, these important details get filled intuitively. Our disputants “get” the fridge. They will disagree strongly. Having successfully used a fridge for decades, surely their model is the correct one? Neither of them have real information on how much energy the fridge spends on cooling the air inside it, and their models give them false confidence.

Many of our opinions form this way as we encounter situations. Finding a way to determine whether an opinion’s source is a quickly filled gap in a simple model helps to discard poor opinions. Did the opinion feel intuitive as soon as it made sense to form it, or do we know why we hold it? Does the person presenting the opinion to you have it because it just “makes sense”, despite being asked for more reasoning?

Nobody holds an opinion which doesn’t make sense from their point of view. If you find yourself unable to explain why someone would believe an opinion besides ad hominems like stupidity or moral corruption, there is likely important missing information. When we doubt our opponent's mental facilities, so too should we doubt our empathy for their point of view.

Disagreements and arguing are pointless if all parties do not come in with a mind willing to change. When we find ourselves repeating the same circle of discourse, we should take another look at how willing everyone is to change their mind. Learning to disengage when we notice another hour of back and forth would lead to the same place is a great way to save an hour of time.
